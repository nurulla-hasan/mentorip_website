Key Legal Remedies in IP Litigation
1. Interim / Temporary Injunctions
Court orders that temporarily restrain infringing activities during ongoing litigation to prevent irreparable harm.
2. Mareva Injunctions
Orders that restrict the disposal or transfer of assets to protect the claimant’s interests during legal proceedings.
3. Anton Piller Orders
Special court orders allowing search and seizure of infringing goods and evidence to prevent destruction or concealment.
4. John Doe Orders
Legal remedies against unknown or unidentified infringers to immediately stop ongoing violations.
5. Permanent Injunctions
Final court orders permanently prohibiting infringing actions.
6. Damages or Account of Profits
Compensation for losses or recovery of illegal profits earned through infringement.
7. Delivery-up & Destruction
Legal removal and destruction of infringing goods and materials.

Disputes & Arbitration

Overview of Arbitration in Bangladesh
Arbitration is an important form of Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) used in Bangladesh to resolve commercial and civil disputes outside traditional court litigation. It is widely used in business contracts to ensure faster, confidential, and cost-effective dispute resolution.
With increasing commercial activities, arbitration has become a preferred mechanism for resolving disputes between parties in Bangladesh.
Legal Framework of Arbitration in Bangladesh
Arbitration in Bangladesh is primarily governed by:
Arbitration Act, 2001 (Bangladesh)
Relevant provisions of Civil Procedure Code (CPC), 1908
Judicial interpretations by the Supreme Court of Bangladesh
The Arbitration Act, 2001 provides the legal structure for both domestic and international arbitration enforcement.
Enforcement of Arbitral Awards in Bangladesh
The enforcement of arbitral awards in Bangladesh depends on whether the award is:
Domestic Arbitral Award
Foreign Arbitral Award

 Enforcement of Domestic Arbitral Awards
Under the Arbitration Act, 2001:
A domestic arbitral award is binding on the parties
It can be enforced as a decree of the civil court
The winning party can apply for enforcement before the competent court
📌 The court will enforce the award unless it is set aside under limited legal grounds such as:
Invalid arbitration agreement
Lack of jurisdiction
Public policy violation
Procedural irregularity
Once confirmed, the award is executed like a civil court judgment.
Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards
Bangladesh is a party to the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards, 1958.
Therefore, foreign arbitral awards are enforceable in Bangladesh under:
Arbitration Act, 2001 (international arbitration provisions)
New York Convention principles
Conditions for Enforcement of Foreign Awards
To enforce a foreign arbitral award in Bangladesh, the applicant must submit:
Original arbitral award or certified copy
Original arbitration agreement or certified copy
Proof that the award falls under a recognized convention
Grounds for Refusal of Enforcement
Bangladesh courts may refuse enforcement if:
The award is against public policy
The arbitration agreement is invalid
Due process was not followed
The award is beyond the scope of arbitration
Court Approach in Bangladesh
Bangladesh courts generally:
Support arbitration and ADR mechanisms
Minimize interference in arbitral awards
Encourage enforcement of valid foreign awards
Promote commercial certainty and investment protection
Importance of Arbitration in Bangladesh
Arbitration plays a key role in:
International trade disputes
Commercial contract enforcement
Construction and infrastructure disputes
Investment and corporate disputes

Anti-Counterfeiting Laws – Bangladesh Context (MentorIP)
What is Counterfeiting?
Counterfeiting means the act of producing, selling, or distributing fake goods by misrepresenting them as genuine products of a well-known brand or manufacturer.
In Bangladesh, counterfeit goods are often sold in a way that misleads consumers into believing they are purchasing original branded products, which directly harms both businesses and consumers.

 Legal Framework in Bangladesh
Anti-counterfeiting and IP protection in Bangladesh are mainly governed by:
Penal Code, 1860
Trademarks Act, 2009
Copyright Act, 2000
Special IP enforcement provisions under civil & criminal law
Customs Act (for import/export control of counterfeit goods)
These laws collectively help in preventing, penalizing, and enforcing action against counterfeit activities.
Impact of Counterfeiting in Bangladesh
Counterfeiting has serious economic and social consequences, including:
Revenue loss for legitimate businesses
Damage to brand reputation and consumer trust
Loss of government tax revenue
Public health risks (especially in pharmaceuticals and food products)
Growth of illegal trade networks
It affects industries such as:
Pharmaceuticals
Cosmetics
Electronics

Software & digital products
Books and media content

Maritime & Admiralty Law – Bangladesh 
Overview of Maritime & Admiralty Law
Maritime and admiralty law deals with legal issues related to shipping, navigation, sea trade, ports, and maritime commercial activities. In modern legal practice, the terms “maritime law” and “admiralty law” are often used interchangeably.
This area of law governs disputes arising from vessels, cargo, marine contracts, shipping operations, and incidents occurring in navigable waters.
Maritime Law in Bangladesh
Bangladesh has a strong maritime legal framework due to its strategic location and active maritime trade through the Bay of Bengal.
Maritime law in Bangladesh primarily covers:
Shipping and vessel registration
Port operations and management
Marine cargo disputes
Ship arrest and maritime claims
Maritime accidents and liability
International shipping regulations
Key Maritime Laws in Bangladesh
Maritime and admiralty matters in Bangladesh are governed by several laws, including:
The Merchant Shipping Ordinance, 1983
The Ports Act, 1908
The Territorial Waters and Maritime Zones Act, 1974
The Inland Shipping Ordinance, 1976
Relevant international maritime conventions adopted by Bangladesh
These laws collectively regulate shipping safety, maritime trade, vessel registration, and dispute resolution.
Admiralty Jurisdiction in Bangladesh
Admiralty jurisdiction in Bangladesh allows courts to handle maritime disputes such as:
Vessel arrest and release
Shipping contract disputes
Cargo damage claims
Marine insurance disputes
Maritime lien enforcement
The High Court Division of the Supreme Court of Bangladesh plays a key role in handling admiralty matters.
Maritime Claims & Disputes
Common maritime disputes in Bangladesh include:
Shipping contract breaches
Freight and cargo disputes
Collision and accident claims
Marine pollution liability
Ship ownership disputes
Charter party disagreements
Maritime Enforcement Mechanism
In Bangladesh, maritime disputes are resolved through:
Admiralty jurisdiction of High Court Division
Civil litigation in commercial courts
Arbitration in shipping contracts
International maritime dispute mechanisms (where applicable)
Courts may order ship arrest, compensation, or injunctions depending on the case.
Importance of Maritime Law in Bangladesh
Maritime law is crucial for Bangladesh because:
It supports international trade and exports
Ensures safety in shipping operations
Protects port infrastructure and cargo
Regulates foreign shipping companies
Maintains maritime security and compliance

 Modern Development

This ensures better enforcement and smoother international maritime operations.
